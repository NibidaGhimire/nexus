1.Introduction
In [12], Filippov introduced the concept of n-Lie algebras, which is diﬀerent from Lie algebras
[16] and plays an important role in mathematical ﬁelds and st ring theory [1, 2, 3]. An n-Lie
algebra is a vector space Awith a linear multiplication (we call it the n-Lie bracket) [ ·,···,·] :
∧nA→Asatisfying the Filippov-Jacobi identity:
[x1,···,xn−1,[y1,···,yn]] =n/summationdisplay
i=1[y1,···,yi−1,[x1,···,xn−1,yi],yi+1,···,yn],
where for all x1,···,xn−1,y1,···,yn∈A. A Poisson n-Lie algebra [9] is a commutative asso-
ciative algebra ( A,·) equipped with an n-Lie bracket, satisfying the generalized Leibniz rule:
[x1,···,xi·˜x,···,xn] =xi·[x1,···,˜x,···,xn]+ ˜x·[x1,···,xi,···,xn],
where for all x1,···,xn,˜x∈A. Poisson n-Lie algebras (also called Nambu-Poisson algebras)
appeared from the study of Nambu mechanics [24] and provided an useful method to describe
algebraical and geometrical structures of Nambu-Poisson m anifolds [25]. Cantarini and Kac
classiﬁed all complex linearly compact Poisson n-Lie algebras in [9].
In recent years, much work has been done in transposed Poisso n algebras ([4, 8, 10, 17, 18,
19, 20, 23, 26]). The transposed Poisson algebra can be gener alized to the transposed Poisson
n-Lie algebra, which consists of a commutative algebra ( A,·) and an n-Lie bracket with n≥2
satisfying:
n˜x·[x1,···,xi,···,xn] =n/summationdisplay
i=1[x1,···,˜x·xi,···,xn],
where for all x1,···,xn,˜x∈A. The diﬀerence between transposed Poisson n-Lie algebras and
Poissonn-Lie algebras lies in the diﬀerent positions of their multipl ications and n-Lie bracket.
In the special case n= 3, transposed Poisson 3-Lie algebras can be deduced not onl y from 3-Lie
algebras and Poisson algebras, but also from 3-Lie algebras and transposed Poisson algebras [4].
