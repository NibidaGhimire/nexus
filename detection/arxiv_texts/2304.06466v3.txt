ABSTRACT 
 
We describe how the market-based average and volatility of the â€œactual â€ return, which the 
investors gain within their market sales, depend on the statistical moments, volatilities, and 
correlations of the current and past market trade values. We describe three successive 
approximations. First, we derive the dependence of the market-based average and volatility 
of a single sale return on market trade statistical moments determined by multiple purchases 
in the past. Then, we describe the dependence of average and volatility of return that a single 
investor gains during the â€œtrading day. â€ Finally, we derive the market-based average and 
volatility of return of different investors during the â€œtrading day" as a function of volatilities 
and correlations of market trade values. That highlights the distribution of the â€œactual â€ return 
of market trade and can serve as a benchmark for â€œpurchasing â€ investors.

6. Conclusion 
This paper describes three successive approximations of the market-based averages 
and volatilities of the â€œactual â€ return that the investors gain during the â€œtrading day. â€ We 
describe the approximations of return generated by a single trade sale, by a single investor , 
and by all investors during the â€œtrading day. â€ We derive the dependence of the market-based 
averages and volatilities on statistical moments, volatilities, and correlations of the current 
and past trade values. Let us highlight some problems that seem to be important for the 
description of financial markets. 
The market-based average and volatility of return that all investors â€œactually â€ gain as 
a result of their sales during the â€œtrading day â€ can serve as benchmarks and impact the 
decisions of â€œpurchasing â€ investors in financial markets. â€œPurchasing â€ investors can assess 
their forecast of the expected returns at horizon T in comparison with the â€œactual â€ returns that  14 â€œselling â€ investors already gain. Analysis of relations between the statistical moments of 
return that â€œselling â€ investors already gain and predictions of the statistical moments of return 
of â€œpurchasing â€ investors can help develop further asset pricing models and portfolio theory. 
The volatility of return (5.8) describes the distribution of the â€œactual â€ returns over numerous 
investors in the stock market.  
Probably, it is difficult to collect and study the market data that permit the 
assessments of market-based averages and volatilities of the â€œactual â€ return of a single 
investor and of all investors during the â€œtrading day. â€ The market data that define the market-
based averages and volatilities of â€œanticipated â€ returns (Section 2) are much more available. 
It is important to study relations between statistical moments of â€œanticipated â€ and â€œactual â€ 
return and highlight possible dependence between these factors.  
The fluctuations of the â€œanticipated â€ returns due to the variations of the time shift Ï„ 
can impact the duration of stock holding by the investors. That, in turn, can change the scales 
and fluctuations of â€œanticipated â€ returns determined by the time shift Ï„. Investigation of the 
hidden mutual dependence between the market-based statistics of the â€œactual â€ return of 
investors and the statistics of the â€œanticipated â€ return can help increase the efficiency of 
portfolio performance and asset pricing models.    15 References
